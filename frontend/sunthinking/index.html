Sun Thinking is a group exhibition that brings together artists, writers and researchers to explore the qualities and logics of solar power and solar powered computing networks. It brings together a collection of network based artworks, games, texts and interviews in the first exhibition project to be hosted on the Solar Protocol network.

In 2020 myself, Alex Nathanson, Benedetta Piantella and a few of our students, all Brooklyn-based artists, designers and researchers, began talking about whether we could design a computer network that was not only powered by the sun, but also programmed by it. Could we subvert those modernist dreams of designing machines to control our environments and instead let our environments control and steer our machines? Could we design in a way that lets the intelligence of the world itself, automate decisions in our technologies rather than the other way around?

With these nascent questions in mind, we began working on Solar Protocol, an artwork in the form of a network of solar-powered web servers that are together, hosting this web platform and all the projects of this show. We started by building and designing a small scale solar power system, we then wrote custom networking software and nurtured collaborations with a diverse and distributed community who have worked with us to install and host the servers in different locations and time zones across the world. The result is many things: it's an experiment in community-run planetary-scale computing, it's a bespoke distributed cloud, it’s a data non-center, and, as this exhibition shows, it's also a virtual, solar powered artist-run space.

Sun Thinking is an exhibition that began with a 2022 open call, from which we commissioned a collection of new work as well as invited the submission of relevant existing projects and texts. The diversity of interlocking questions and concerns that are grappled with here reflects the unruly, messy and transdisciplinary nature of the Solar Protocol project itself. These include works and texts that ask questions of intelligence and reveal that what counts as intelligence has never been fixed and is therefore open for reconsideration. Many of the texts and interviews consider the practices and politics of community networks and challenges of self hosting infrastructure, many also draw attention to the materiality and situatedness of data and computing and how to design and live within planetary limits and boundaries. Finally, many projects, as well as the design of the exhibition site itself, explore small data aesthetics or work with the rhythms and cyclical nature of solar energy and in doing so probe the characteristics and qualities of working with solar powered computing. You also might notice that the exhibition website changes colors at different times of the day which occurs as the site is generated with parameters specific to each of the physical servers, and that have been set by each of the Solar Protocol stewards. Like the environments we share, the design of this website is ever changing and reflects dynamism of the place where it is situated.
